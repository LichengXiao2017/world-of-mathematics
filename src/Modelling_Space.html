Euclidean Geometry

Euclid of Alexandria (around 300 BC)

When deriving mathematical results and principles we need axioms or postulates, a collection of definitions and observations which we assume to be true and which make up our logical arguments.

The five basic axioms of geometry were established by Euclid of Alexandria, one of the greatest ancient Greek mathematicians. They can be expressed in many different ways, for example the ones below:

AXIOM 1
You can join any two points using exactly one straight line.

AXIOM 2
You can extend any line segment to an infinite line.

AXIOM 3
Given a point P and a distance r, you can draw a circle with centre P and radius r.

AXIOM 4
Any two right angles are congruent.

AXIOM 5
Given a line L and a point P not on L, there is exactly one (parallel) line which goes through P and never meets L.

The fifth axiom, the Parallel postulate, is certainly the least intuitive axiom, and ancient mathematicians debated whether we can just assume it without proof. Today we know that different sets of initial axioms simply give rise to different kinds of geometry.

Every result and theorem in (Euclidean) Geometry can be proved using nothing but the five axioms above. One example is the proof that the three angles in a triangle always add up to 180°.

Pythagoras

Pythagoras of Samos (around 570 – 495 BC)

The most famous geometrical result is Pythagoras’ Theorem, the statement of which is simple and beautiful: “The square of the hypothenuse (the longest side) in a right angled triangle is the sum of the square of the other two sides”, or equivalently a2 + b2 = c2.

The formula was known to ancient Babylonians, Mesopotamians, Indians and Chinese, but the Greek mathematician Pythagoras of Samos may have been the first to find a proof.

Much about Pythagoras’ life is unknown, and no copies of his original writings have survived. He founded a religious cult – the Pythagoreans – which was based on philosophy and mathematics, and the belief that numbers underly nature and the universe. Among many other things, the Pythagoreans are credited with discovering the first irrational number, √2, a notion which they found deeply troubling.

Pythagoras’ Theorem still works if we replace all squares above by regular pentagons or equilateral triangles. It also works in three dimensions as a2 + b2 + c2 = d2. The theorem only holds in Euclidean space – in fact it is equivalent to the Parallel postulate which we saw above.

The Pythagorean Theorem gives rise to sets of three numbers a, b and c. If these are all integers they are called a Pythagorean Triple. For example, (3, 4, 5) is a Pythagorean triple because 32 + 42 = 9 + 16 = 25 = 52.

If we multiply every number by a factor of 2 we get another Pythagorean triple, (6, 8, 10). Other triples include (5, 12, 13) or (8, 15, 17).

Euclid found a simple formula for generating new Pythagorean triples: take any two integers m = V.PythM and n = V.PythN and calculate twice their product, the difference of their squares, and the sum of their squares:

a = 2 × V.PythM × V.PythN = 2 * V.PythM * V.PythN b = Math.max(V.PythM,V.PythN)2 – Math.min(V.PythM,V.PythN)2 = Math.abs(V.PythM*V.PythM - V.PythN*V.PythN) c = V.PythM2 + V.PythN2 = V.PythM*V.PythM + V.PythN*V.PythN
